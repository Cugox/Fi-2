A Spell of Good Things

The novel explores the themes of poverty, abuse, bad leadership, political thuggery, love, and sisterhood.

A Spell of Good Things tells the story of Eniola Oni and Wuraola Makinde by juxtaposing their lives to show the reality of social inequality.

The Importance of Sisterhood | A Review of A Spell of Good Things by Ayobami Adebayo

September 17, 2024

A striking accomplishment of A Spell of Good Things is its reflective and refractive ability. It not only shows the ills that taint our society but also points to the repercussions while emphasizing the urgency of fixing them. Lack and poverty are explored through society’s relegation of empathy, the implications of empowering greedy and apathetic politicians like Honourable Fesojaiye, and the systemic failure that grows inequality and injustice. The failure of modern Nigeria is not loosely interrogated here, as done in some novels. It is done through the eyes of society’s smallest unit (the family) with a careful reflection of disintegration.

Adébáyọ̀ projects poverty in the life of Eniola and his family with harrowing images that pierce the heart of readers. When Eniola and Busola are not being whipped for their parent’s failure to pay fees, their mother is either out begging for assistance or ‘probably searching dunghills for used plastics and bottles she could resell’, or her painful decision to pick a child’s education over the other as the family’s collective effort cannot keep both in school. More pity is evoked with Eniola’s father whose slow decline hurts his family and readers. His good days have become faint memories for his wife and children. Resigned to fate, he lives his days in silence, bereft of the resources that once made him a proud husband and a good father, also bereft of words. His powerlessness in the painful decimation of his family initiates the conversation on the place and responsibility of government, power and greed, the prioritization of a protection scheme for the vulnerable and the moral burden in the duty of the society’s elite to the downtrodden.

A remarkable exploit of Adébáyọ̀’s latest novel is the language that invites readers to its geographical setting. Adébáyọ̀ richly elevates the Yoruba language as a tool of narration while paying keen attention to the language’s diacritics. The Yoruba language serves the writer’s intent in delivering a Nigerian story that is not tied to urban Lagos. In names, food, dialogue and its portrayal of the Yoruba folklore, A Spell of Good Things centres on the peculiarities of modern Nigeria through the lens of a Yoruba town. Without considering the temptation of economics and the wider readership offered by the West, the novel invites everyone to its locale by not providing clues such as the translation of Yoruba expressions, the italicization of Yoruba words and the linguistic Westernization of Yoruba cuisine and traditional concepts.

The nation eventually tires of The Old Horse and particularly his wife, and there is a military coup, led by Tuvius Shasha, the former Vice President. The Old Horse goes into exile. The situation for the country does not improve, however. The economy in fact worsens still further and discontent abounds. Enter Destiny Lozikeyi, a gentle female goat who fled her village many years earlier but who has now returned to search for her family and her history. She shows her fellow citizens how desperate their situation is and, slowly, a citizen-led uprising begins.

Enunciated through the thoughtfulness in Adébáyọ̀’s creativity, A Spell of Good Things is more than a cautionary tale. It is a successful attempt at showing who we are, where we are, what needs to be done and how close social classes are to each other when the numbers say otherwise. It is a sobering tale that spares no one

Conclusion

Overall, I enjoyed how Adebayo explores the theme of sisterhood in the novel. It could serve as a catalyst for women to build more valuable relationships with other women because it’s only women who truly understand women’s plights. Also, by doing this, she deconstructs the notion that women are each other’s enemies.
